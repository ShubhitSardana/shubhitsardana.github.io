Cosmic Consciousness: Are we all Trippin?

Advait Vedanta · Mandukya Upnishad · Psychedelics

What if an ancient Indian scripture and a modern psychedelic unlocked the same door—the ultimate nature of reality?

I recently explored accounts of experiences with DMT (N,N-Dimethyltryptamine), a powerful psychoactive substance. What's astonishing is how deeply these modern narratives mirror the profound descriptions of consciousness found in ancient Indian yogic and Vedantic texts, especially the Mandukya Upanishad. It seems as though two distinct paths—one rooted in modern chemistry, the other in ancient philosophy—might converge upon the same timeless truth.

The DMT Journey and the Echoes of Non-Duality

Individuals who have journeyed with DMT often describe a radical dissolution of the self, an overwhelming sensation of oneness with the universe, or a profound merging with a universal "source." They speak of "waking up" from our perceived reality, realizing our world is more dream-like than we believe, and attaining a state of ineffable bliss. These are not mere poetic metaphors; they resonate with the core tenets of Advaita Vedanta, a school of Hindu philosophy. Advaita Vedanta teaches that the individual self or soul (Atman) and the ultimate reality (Brahman) are not separate entities but are, in fact, one and the same. According to this philosophy, the illusion of separation, a concept known as Maya, is what prevents us from perceiving this fundamental unity.

The Four States of Consciousness: The Mandukya Upanishad

The Mandukya Upanishad, though comprising only twelve verses, masterfully dissects this truth by exploring the four states of consciousness the Atman experiences. These states, or avasthas, provide a framework for understanding the layers of our reality.

1. Vaishvanara (The Waking State): This is the state of outward-turned consciousness, where we perceive the external world through our senses. The Upanishad describes this as bahish-prajna (outward-knowing). (Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 3)

2. Taijasa (The Dreaming State): Here, awareness is turned inward, experiencing a world constructed of mental impressions and memories. This is the state of antah-prajna (inward-knowing). (Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 4)

3. Prajna (The Deep Sleep State): In this state, there are no dreams and no objects of perception. It is a state of undifferentiated consciousness, a seed of awareness without division. (Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 5)

The common thread through these three states is the persistent, underlying awareness of existence—the fundamental feeling of "I am."

4. Turiya (The Fourth): This is not truly a state but the foundational reality that underlies and transcends the other three. It cannot be perceived by the senses, described in language, or conceived by the mind. It is beyond all duality and is identified as the true Self (Atman).

The Mandukya Upanishad describes Turiya with breathtaking clarity:

It is not that which is conscious of the internal subjective world, nor that which is conscious of the external world, nor that which is conscious of both, nor that which is a mass of consciousness, nor that which is simple consciousness, nor is it unconsciousness. It is unseen by any sense-organ, beyond empirical dealings, incomprehensible by the mind, uninferable, unthinkable, indescribable, essentially by of the Self alone, negation of all phenomena, the peaceful, the auspicious and the nondual. This is what is considered as the Fourth (Turiya). This is the Atman and this is to be realised.” (Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 7)

Modern psychology and neuroscience might label the dissolution of self often experienced with DMT as "ego death." Vedanta, on the other hand, describes the realization of one's true nature as Self-realization (Atma-jnana), a process that inherently involves transcending the limited ego. Perhaps these are simply two different lenses—one chemical, one conscious—observing the same ineffable truth.

The "Spirit Molecule" and the Brain's Mysteries

Interestingly, scientific research confirms that the human body can naturally produce DMT. Its exact purpose as an endogenous compound remains a topic of scientific inquiry. Speculative theories, notably from Dr. Rick Strassman, author of "DMT: The Spirit Molecule," propose that elevated levels of DMT might be released during pivotal life moments such as birth and death, and potentially during deep meditative states. While evidence has been found for increased DMT concentrations in the brains of rodents during cardiac arrest, there is currently no conclusive scientific proof that meditation significantly increases DMT production in humans, or that the pineal gland produces enough of it to cause psychoactive effects. Still, this has led to a fascinating hypothesis: what if our normal waking consciousness is a form of filtered reality, and substances like DMT temporarily disable this filter? Could the brain's natural regulatory mechanisms be analogous to the Vedantic concept of Maya?

The Riddle of Oneness and Individuality

This line of thought raises a profound paradox. If a few individuals can use a substance like DMT to access a "higher" plane of reality, what does that imply for the rest of humanity? If our reality is akin to a dream from which one can "wake up," are the rest of us merely non-player characters (NPCs) in that individual's dream? How can we all be one unified entity, as Advaita Vedanta's core principle of non-duality (Advaita literally means "not two") asserts, if some can escape this shared reality while others remain?

An Example for Computer Geeks: To grapple with this, a modern analogy might be helpful: a powerful computer running multiple virtual machines (VMs). In essence, all the VMs are expressions of the same underlying hardware, but from their individual perspectives, they operate as separate and distinct systems. If these VMs could communicate, they would likely be unaware of their fundamental interconnection. The virtualization software creates the illusion of separation.

If the ultimate reality, Brahman, is present in everything, and in fact, is everyone and everything, then when a person experiences enlightenment, they are essentially freed from the "illusion" of being a separate VM and merge back into the main operating system—the source code of existence itself.

Maya and Leela: The Divine Play of Consciousness

The Upanishads offer another beautiful perspective through the concepts of Maya and Leela. Brahman's creative power, Maya, is the very force that veils the singular reality, making the One appear as the many. This entire cosmic unfolding is described as Leela, or divine play. The Taittiriya Upanishad suggests that in the beginning, the Absolute was alone and desired, "May I be many, may I grow forth." (Taittiriya Upanishad, 2.6.1) Through this divine play, Brahman experiences its own infinite potential through a myriad of forms. We, as individual nodes of consciousness, gather a universe of experiences—joy, sorrow, love, and grief. Upon enlightenment, these experiences are re-integrated into the whole of Brahman, enriching the cosmic consciousness. In essence, we are the co-creators of our reality, temporarily veiled from our true, all-encompassing Self. The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad beautifully states, "This (self) was indeed Brahman in the beginning. It knew only Itself as, 'I am Brahman'. Therefore It became all." (Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, 1.4.10)

Perhaps, then, we are not discovering something new at all, but simply—and profoundly—remembering what we have always been.
